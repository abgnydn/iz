Submission to the EU consultation on the CBAM default-values implementing act (May 2026)

The EU's 2026 default emission values for Turkish cement, EAF steel, and N₂O-controlled fertilizer overshoot the audit-grade truth by 2–40×. We submit empirical counter-defaults derived from 21 audit-grade Turkish facilities, 789 EU-verified facilities under the EU ETS (~11,700 audited plant-years from the EU Transaction Log), and an independently rediscovered closed-form formula, asking the Commission to move away from 90th-percentile global anchors toward sector-mean defaults with operator opt-in.

1. Summary

The 2026 CBAM definitive phase applies country- and product-specific default emission values to imports for which operators do not submit verified actual data. The current implementing regulation derives these defaults from the 90th percentile of global emissions intensity per route and adds a markup escalating to 30% by 2028 — a conservative anchor designed to push operators toward submitting their own data. We do not contest the markup logic. We do contest the empirical anchor.

On the four CBAM scopes relevant to Turkey, the 90th-percentile anchor is wrong in both directions:

  • Cement (Turkey, Portland clinker): EU default 1.584 tCO₂/t × 1.10 markup ≈ 1.74 t/t in 2026. Audit-grade TR sector mean (TÜRKÇİMENTO) is 0.643 t/t. Verified Turkish operator audits (Akçansa Büyükçekmece 2025, Çimsa Mersin 2024, OYAK Group 2023) cluster within ±15% of that mean. The default is 2.7× too high.
  • EAF steel: EU default 1.900 tCO₂/t × 1.10 ≈ 2.09 t/t. Turkish EAF actual: 0.25 t/t direct Scope 1 (Çolakoğlu 2024, Habaş 2024, İzdemir 2024). The default is 8.4× too high for the dominant Turkish steel route.
  • Primary aluminium (Scope 1 only): EU CBAM Annex VIII Scope-1-only default is 1.471 t/t. EUTL verified Scope 1 across 31 EU primary smelters averages ≈ 2.14 t/t (5.77 Mt / 2.7 Mt production). The default undershoots by 31% — the only CBAM route where this happens.
  • Fertilizer (N₂O-controlled nitric acid): EU default 2.351 tCO₂/t × 1.01 (annual fertilizer markup) ≈ 2.37 t/t. BAGFAŞ Bandırma 2024 (TR's only N₂O-catalyst-equipped operator): 0.05 t/t. The default is 47× too high, eliminating the carbon-price incentive for installing N₂O abatement.

Proposal in one sentence

Replace the 90th-percentile global anchor with a route-specific median of audit-grade operator-disclosed Scope 1 from the importing country (where available), retaining the existing markup schedule but applied to the empirically defensible anchor instead of a worst-case stand-in. We provide the empirical anchors for Turkey below and the open data + reproducible methodology to extend them to other importing countries.

2. Empirical basis

2.1 TR-MRV-Bench — 21 audit-grade Turkish plants

The TR-MRV-Bench is an open dataset of 59 CBAM-scope Turkish facilities (32 cement, 16 steel, 3 aluminum, 6 fertilizer, 2 lime-only). Each plant has nameplate capacity (operator-published, cross-referenced with industry registries), CN-code mapping, lat/lon, and disclosure URL. Of those, 21 plants have audit-grade Scope 1 emissions sourced from operator Integrated Annual Reports, TSRS-compliant sustainability statements, and ISO 14064-1 audit reports verified by KGK-accredited or TÜRKAK-accredited third parties. Provenance is tagged on every label: direct, allocated (from group total via clinker share), or composite (multi-site total).

In leave-one-disclosure-out (LODO) cross-validation, a closed-form physics formula tCO₂ = capacity × route-EF × capacity-factor achieves log-MAE 0.211 against audited truth, compared with the EU CBAM default's log-MAE of 1.432 on the same 21 plants — an 85.3% reduction. The formula uses three inputs every Turkish operator already publishes in their annual report: nameplate capacity (KAP filings, industry registry), an industry-standard route-EF (TÜRKÇİMENTO sector mean for cement; route-specific for steel BF/BOF / EAF / DRI-EAF; primary vs downstream for aluminum; integrated vs N₂O-controlled vs blender for fertilizer), and operator-disclosed production tonnage divided by capacity (capacity factor, available in the same IARs).

2.2 EU-EUTL external validation — 789 plants × ~11,700 audit-grade plant-years

To answer the obvious "this is just calibrated for Turkey" critique, we extracted verified Scope 1 emissions from the EU Transaction Log for every EU ETS installation in the four CBAM scopes: 372 cement clinker installations, 282 pig-iron-and-steel installations, 71 primary + secondary aluminum installations, 64 ammonia + nitric-acid installations. Each is third-party verified annually under EU ETS Article 15. Across 2005–latest reported year, this is approximately 11,700 audit-grade Scope 1 facility-years.

The conclusion is uncomfortable for both directions: the EU CBAM defaults are systematically wrong against EU's own audit-grade data, in directions that depend on sector. Cement defaults overshoot EU truth by 2.55× (the EU's own plants would be over-tariffed by default). Primary aluminium Scope-1 defaults undershoot truth by 31%. A consistent methodology for setting defaults would address both.

2.3 Independent algorithmic rediscovery — PySR symbolic regression

To answer the "you engineered this multiplicative form to fit your bench" critique, we ran evolutionary symbolic regression (PySR; Cranmer 2023) over the combined TR + EU dataset of 58 audit-grade plants with capacity, route-EF, and capacity-factor as the only inputs. Operators allowed: + − × ÷ log exp. No prior on the formula structure.

At complexity 5, the log-space Pareto front contains:

(log_cap + log_ef) + log_cf

algebraically identical to log(capacity × route-EF × capacity-factor). PySR independently rediscovers the hand-crafted formula structure at the same loss (0.59) with no human guidance. At higher complexity the algorithm finds a tighter fit with a small EF-CF coupling correction — a potential refinement direction we flag but do not adopt for v0.

4. Critique of the 90th-percentile methodology

The Commission's choice of a 90th-percentile global anchor was a reasonable starting position when verified per-country data did not exist. As of 2026 it does — for cement, EU ETS Article 15 verified data covers 90%+ of European production; for Turkey, audit-grade IARs cover 70%+ of CBAM-relevant capacity; for ammonia and primary aluminium, EUTL covers the entire EU production. Persisting with the 90th-percentile anchor produces three pathological outcomes:

  1. Mis-pricing carbon-route decisions. The 47× over-tariff for N₂O-controlled nitric acid eliminates the financial case for installing N₂O abatement — exactly the technology the EU's ETS has been trying to incentivise since 2013. Operators who already installed the catalyst (BAGFAŞ being the TR example; YARA Sluiskil being the EU peer) are tariffed identically to plants that have not. The default discourages, rather than rewards, the abatement choice.
  2. Penalising the dominant clean route. Turkish steel exports to the EU are 95% EAF (scrap-fed, electricity-intensive, low direct Scope 1). The 8.4× over-tariff on EAF steel exports €X bn/yr of Turkish scrap-steel manufacturing to a tariff calibrated for the integrated BF/BOF route they don't use.
  3. Hiding the under-tariff problem. Primary aluminium Scope-1 default 1.471 t/t undershoots EUTL truth by 31%. The 90th-percentile-of-global anchor for aluminium is dragged down by best-in-class non-EU smelters (Iceland, UAE) on hydropower with modern anodes. EU's own fleet emits more Scope 1 because of older potlines. The default is structurally too low for the EU and structurally too low for Turkey's only primary smelter (Eti Alüminyum Seydişehir).

The fundamental issue is that "90th percentile of global emissions intensity" is calibrated to penalise data-shy operators by making the default unattractive. That goal is preserved by the 10/20/30% markup. The percentile-90 anchor adds a second layer of pessimism that is empirically unsound and stops being needed once verified per-country data exists.

5. Recommended changes to the implementing act

  1. Article 4(3) and Annex II: replace 90th-percentile-of-global anchor with the median of audit-grade operator-disclosed Scope 1 from the importing country, where ≥ 5 operators with third-party-verified disclosures cover ≥ 50% of CBAM-relevant capacity for that route. Retain the 10/20/30% markup schedule.
  2. Annex VIII (aluminium): replace the 1.471 t/t Scope-1-only default with the median of EUTL primary-aluminium verified Scope 1 (currently ≈ 2.14 t/t across 31 EU smelters). The Commission's own audit-grade data should anchor its own defaults.
  3. Sub-route differentiation: split EAF / BF/BOF / DRI-EAF defaults for steel; split primary / downstream defaults for aluminium; split integrated / N₂O-controlled / blender for fertilizer. The current single-default-per-CN-code structure conflates 30–80× spreads within sector.
  4. Verifier accreditation: third-country MRV-verifier accreditation by TÜRKAK (Turkey), DAkkS (Germany), UKAS (UK) etc. should be recognised as equivalent to EU-accredited verification for the purpose of submitting actual values, provided the verifier follows ISO 14064-1.
  5. Open-data anchor publication: the Commission should publish the underlying anchor data (per-operator, per-year, per-route) as machine-readable CSV with a stable URL, in the same way that EUTL is published, so the empirical basis of every default is independently verifiable.

6. What we commit to provide

Should the Commission find this submission useful, we commit to maintain TR-MRV-Bench under Apache-2.0 with annual refresh of audit-grade labels each April (following the Turkish IAR publication cycle), to provide the same per-route audit-grade extraction for any other importing country whose operator IARs we can crawl (we currently have partial coverage of Greece, Romania, Spain via Holcim/Titan/Cementos Argos disclosures), and to keep the formula and the LODO methodology in the public domain. All code, all data, all formula derivations are at github.com/abgnydn/iz under Apache-2.0.
